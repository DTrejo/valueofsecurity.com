Yes, "per violation" is a term that can be terrifyingly broad. In the eyes of a regulator like the FTC or HHS (for HIPAA), it doesn't always just mean "one day of being bad."

### Does "Per Violation" Mean Per Person?
**Yes and no.** It depends on the specific law being applied, but the trend is moving toward "per person."

* **FTC Act:** Historically, the FTC viewed a "violation" as a single act (e.g., one deceptive advertisement). However, in modern data privacy cases, they have increasingly argued that **each individual consumer** affected by a deceptive practice constitutes a separate violation.
* **HIPAA:** For healthcare, it is explicitly tied to individuals. If 500,000 records are exposed, that can be viewed as 500,000 violations.
* **The "Daily" Modifier:** If the violation is considered "continuing" (like failing to implement a required firewall), regulators often charge **per day**. As of 2026, the maximum civil penalty for violating an FTC order is **$53,088 per violation/day**.

### Industry-Specific Governance
Different industries face different oversight "flavors" when they fail a security audit or suffer a breach.

| Industry | Primary Regulator | Governance Mechanism | Core Focus |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Finance** | SEC / OCC / DFS | **Consent Order / Cease & Desist** | Focuses on "Internal Controls" and the accuracy of risk disclosures to shareholders. |
| **Healthcare** | HHS (OCR) | **Resolution Agreement & CAP** | The "Corrective Action Plan" (CAP) usually lasts 2–3 years and requires granular reporting on encryption and employee training. |
| **Tech/Retail** | FTC | **Consent Decree** | Usually lasts 20 years. Focuses on "Unfair and Deceptive" practices and requires biennial independent audits. |

### The Stakes: Initial Order vs. The "Second Mistake"
The government is often relatively "lenient" on a first offense—the goal is remediation. But once you are under an order, the legal standard changes from "negligence" to **"contempt."**

| Stage | Legal Characterization | Typical Monetary Impact | Oversight Level |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Initial Consent Order** | Remedial / Settlement | Often $0 to $5M (depending on harm). Focus is on *fixing* the problem. | **Moderate:** You report to the agency. You must hire an auditor. |
| **2nd Mistake (Under Order)** | **Willful Violation / Contempt** | **Massive.** Fines often jump to $50M+ or even billions (e.g., Facebook’s $5B fine was due to violating a previous order). | **Extreme:** The government may appoint a "Monitor" who sits in your offices and vets every decision. |

### Why the "Second Mistake" is a Company-Killer
When a company violates an existing consent order, they lose their "good faith" standing. 
1.  **Strict Liability:** The government no longer has to prove you were "unfair"—they only have to prove you didn't follow the specific checklist in the order.
2.  **Officer Liability:** In recent years (post-2023), the FTC has begun naming **individual CEOs and CISOs** in these orders. This means the executives can be held personally liable for future security failures.



> **Note on 2026 Caps:** As of this year, the inflation-adjusted cap for most federal civil penalties sits around **$53,000**. If a company loses data for 1,000,000 people and the government charges "per person," the theoretical fine is $53 Billion. While they rarely charge the max, the "per person" math gives the government massive leverage in settlement negotiations.
