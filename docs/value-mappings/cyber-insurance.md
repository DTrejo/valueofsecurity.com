Based on the latest research, how much does cyber insurance cost per industry and as it relates to revenue and public/private? Also, for the first breach vs 2nd.

---

In 2026, the cyber insurance market is experiencing a "rare divergence": while claim frequency and severity are at record highs, premiums have largely stabilized or seen moderate decreases due to an influx of new insurers and increased competition. However, this "buyer-friendly" phase is localized primarily to companies with high-security maturity.

The following data reflects current 2026 benchmarks for premiums, industry variations, and the impact of repeat claims.

---

### **2. Cost Relative to Revenue**
Revenue is the primary "rating lever" insurers use to calculate exposure. As of 2026, the cost per million dollars of revenue has shifted:

* **Micro-Businesses (<$1M Revenue):** Typically pay flat fees ranging from **$500 to $1,500** annually.
* **Mid-Market ($10M – $250M Revenue):** Average premiums sit around **$17,600** (roughly **$70 to $150 per $1M of revenue**).
* **Large Enterprise (>$1B Revenue):** Premiums are highly customized but often average **$1,000 to $5,000 per $1M of revenue**, depending on the retention (deductible) and security controls.

---

### **3. Public vs. Private Companies**
Public companies generally face **15% to 30% higher premiums** than private companies of equivalent size.
* **Public Companies:** Insurers charge more due to **Securities & Exchange Commission (SEC)** disclosure requirements and the higher risk of **Stock Drop Litigation** or class-action lawsuits following a breach.
* **Private Companies:** Benefit from less regulatory scrutiny and lower legal defense costs, though this gap is narrowing as privacy regulations (like GDPR/CCPA) apply to both.

---

### **4. Impact of Breaches: 1st vs. 2nd Claims**
A claim doesn't just increase your price; it changes your insurability.

* **After the 1st Breach:** * **Premium Increase:** Expect a **20% to 50% hike** at renewal.
    * **Requirement:** Insurers will typically mandate a "Post-Incident Remediation Report." If you cannot prove that the specific vulnerability was patched (e.g., implementing MFA), they may refuse to renew entirely.
* **After the 2nd Breach:** * **Premium Increase:** Often **100%+ (doubling)**, or the policy may be moved to the "Surplus Lines" (non-standard) market where costs are significantly higher.
    * **Coverage Restrictions:** You will likely see "sub-limits" (e.g., your $5M policy now only covers $500k for ransomware) or much higher deductibles.
    * **Denial Risk:** In 2026, approximately **27% of claims** are partially or fully denied if the company failed to maintain the security controls promised in their application.

---

### **2026 "Must-Haves" for Lower Premiums**
Insurers now view certain tech as non-negotiable. Missing these can lead to a **50% premium penalty** or a flat denial:
1.  **Phishing-Resistant MFA:** Standard MFA (SMS/Push) is no longer enough for high-risk industries.
2.  **Endpoint Detection & Response (EDR):** 24/7 monitoring is now a baseline requirement.
3.  **Immutable Backups:** Backups must be "air-gapped" or unchangeable to protect against ransomware.
